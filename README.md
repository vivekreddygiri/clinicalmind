## Overview

ClinicalMind is an AI-powered clinical decision support system (CDSS) built on the MIMIC-III critical care database. It addresses a fundamental gap in modern healthcare — the inability to rapidly synthesize a patient's complex clinical history and proactively assess chronic disease risk at the point of care.

The system operates as a multi-modal pipeline combining **Natural Language Processing** for clinical note summarization with **Gradient Boosted Tree Models** for risk prediction, augmented by **Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP)** for clinical interpretability. Unlike black-box clinical AI systems, ClinicalMind provides feature-level explanations for every prediction in both visual and natural language form.

The complete pipeline processes a patient's Electronic Health Record (EHR) — spanning diagnoses, laboratory results, medication history, procedures, and free-text clinical notes — and delivers a structured clinical intelligence report covering:

- A comprehensive clinical journey narrative generated by BART-Large-CNN
- Risk probability scores for Type 2 Diabetes, Chronic Kidney Disease, and Heart Failure
- SHAP-based feature attribution explaining the primary drivers of each prediction
- Longitudinal risk trajectory tracking across multiple hospital admissions
- Current symptom integration using ICD-9 coded symptom features

---

## Problem Statement

Electronic Health Records contain rich clinical information distributed across structured tables (diagnoses, labs, medications) and unstructured free-text (physician notes, discharge summaries). Clinical staff face three core challenges:

**1. Information overload** — An ICU patient's record may span dozens of admissions, hundreds of lab results, and thousands of words of clinical notes. No physician can efficiently synthesize this at the point of care.

**2. Reactive rather than proactive care** — Chronic diseases like Diabetes, CKD, and Heart Failure are frequently diagnosed late because no systematic risk screening is applied to existing patient data.

**3. Black-box AI distrust** — Existing clinical AI models produce risk scores without explanation. Clinicians cannot — and should not — act on predictions they cannot interpret or verify.

ClinicalMind addresses all three directly: automated summarization addresses information overload, risk prediction enables proactive screening, and SHAP explainability enables clinical trust and verification.

## Key Features

### 🔬 Multi-Modal Clinical Pipeline
Combines unstructured NLP (clinical notes) with structured tabular ML (labs, medications, diagnoses) — mirroring real-world clinical AI architecture.

### 📋 Detailed Clinical Summarization
BART-Large-CNN generates structured summaries covering diagnosis history, medication profile, procedures, lab value trends, and a clinical narrative from free-text notes. Notes are prioritized by clinical relevance (discharge summaries > physician notes > nursing notes) and chunked to handle BART's 1024-token limit.

### 🎯 Three-Disease Risk Prediction
Independent XGBoost binary classifiers predict risk for:
- **Type 2 Diabetes** — glucose trends, HbA1c, insulin/metformin prescriptions
- **Chronic Kidney Disease** — creatinine, BUN, eGFR proxies, dialysis procedures
- **Heart Failure** — BNP levels, diuretic/beta-blocker prescriptions, cardiac ICD codes

### 💡 Explainable AI with Natural Language
SHAP TreeExplainer computes per-feature attribution for each patient prediction. Attributions are automatically converted to plain English explanations, making the system interpretable to non-technical clinical staff.

### 📈 Longitudinal Risk Tracking
Risk scores are computed independently for each hospital admission and plotted as a time-series — revealing whether a patient's clinical trajectory is improving or deteriorating across visits.

### 🩺 Data-Driven Symptom Integration
Current symptoms selected by a physician are mapped to ICD-9 coded symptom features (780–799) that were trained as part of the XGBoost feature set. Symptom weights are learned from 46,520 patient records — not manually assigned.

### 🔍 Ablation Study
Full three-way comparison against Logistic Regression and Random Forest baselines with 5-fold cross-validation, confirming XGBoost as the superior choice for this clinical dataset.

## Methodology

### 1. Clinical Summarization

Clinical notes from `NOTEEVENTS` are preprocessed by removing MIMIC-III anonymization tags (`[** ... **]`), collapsing whitespace, and prioritizing note categories in order of clinical information density:

```
Priority 1: Discharge Summary
Priority 2: Physician Notes
Priority 3: Nursing Notes
Priority 4: Radiology Reports
Priority 5: ECG / Echo Reports
```

Long note sequences are chunked into 900-token segments (below BART's 1024-token limit) and each chunk is independently summarized. The chunk summaries are concatenated into a final clinical narrative.

**Model:** `facebook/bart-large-cnn` — a sequence-to-sequence transformer pre-trained on CNN/DailyMail corpus with bidirectional encoder and autoregressive decoder.

**Generation parameters:** `num_beams=4`, `max_length=200`, `min_length=60`, `early_stopping=True`

In addition to the neural narrative, structured sections are extracted via rule-based methods:
- **Medications** — regex pattern matching over 14 common drug families
- **Procedures** — keyword matching against 15 clinical procedure terms
- **Lab highlights** — statistical aggregates (mean, max, min, std) per lab test

---

### 2. Feature Engineering

Each patient is represented as an 82-dimensional feature vector:

**Demographics (2 features)**
- Age at first admission (computed year-level to avoid MIMIC-III overflow)
- Gender (binary encoded)

**Admission Statistics (4 features)**
- Number of hospital admissions
- Average length of stay (days)
- Maximum length of stay (days)
- Emergency admission count

**Laboratory Features (50 features)**
Ten key lab tests × five statistics (mean, max, min, std, count):

| Lab Test | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|
| Glucose | Primary diabetes biomarker |
| Creatinine | Kidney function indicator |
| Blood Urea Nitrogen | Kidney function indicator |
| HbA1c | Long-term glycemic control |
| BNP | Heart failure biomarker |
| Hemoglobin | Anemia and general health |
| Potassium | Electrolyte balance |
| Sodium | Fluid and electrolyte status |
| Platelets | Hematological health |
| WBC | Infection and inflammatory state |

**Prescription Flags (7 features)**
Binary indicators for medication class presence:
Insulin, Metformin, Diuretics, ACE Inhibitors, Statins, Beta-Blockers, Dialysis

**Diagnosis Comorbidity Flags (6 features)**
Binary indicators for: Hypertension, Obesity, Anemia, COPD, Liver Disease, Unique Diagnosis Count

**Symptom Features (12 features)**
Binary indicators derived from ICD-9 symptom codes (780–799):
Fatigue, Frequent Urination, Excessive Thirst, Chest Pain, Shortness of Breath, Swollen Legs, Blurred Vision, Nausea, Decreased Urine Output, Rapid Weight Gain, Dizziness, Palpitations

---

### 3. Risk Prediction Models

Three independent XGBoost binary classifiers are trained — one per disease target. This multi-model approach is preferred over multi-label classification because each disease has distinct feature importance profiles and different class imbalance ratios.

**Architecture Parameters:**

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `n_estimators` | 300 | Sufficient trees without overfitting |
| `max_depth` | 6 | Controls model complexity |
| `learning_rate` | 0.05 | Conservative learning for stability |
| `subsample` | 0.8 | Row subsampling prevents overfitting |
| `colsample_bytree` | 0.8 | Feature subsampling adds robustness |
| `scale_pos_weight` | Dynamic | Corrects class imbalance per disease |
| `eval_metric` | AUC | Optimizes ranking quality |

**Class Imbalance Handling:**
`scale_pos_weight = count(negative) / count(positive)` — computed independently per disease. For CKD (12% prevalence), this yields approximately 7.3, meaning each positive case is weighted as 7.3 negative cases during gradient computation.

**Label Generation:**
Risk labels are derived from ICD-9 codes in `DIAGNOSES_ICD`:

| Disease | ICD-9 Prefixes | Clinical Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Type 2 Diabetes | 250.xx | Diabetes Mellitus family |
| CKD | 585, 403, 404 | Chronic Kidney Disease + Hypertensive variants |
| Heart Failure | 428, 402 | Heart Failure + Hypertensive Heart Disease |

---

### 4. Symptom Integration

Symptoms selected by a physician at the point of care are mapped to their corresponding ICD-9 symptom feature columns and set to 1 in the patient feature vector before prediction. This approach is data-driven — the model learned the association between each symptom and each disease from the training population.

This differs fundamentally from heuristic approaches where weights are manually assigned. The symptom weights embedded in the XGBoost model reflect true statistical associations across 46,520 ICU patients.

**ICD-9 Symptom Code Mapping:**

| Symptom | ICD-9 Codes |
|---|---|
| Fatigue | 780.79, 780.7 |
| Frequent Urination | 788.41, 788.42 |
| Chest Pain | 786.50, 786.51, 786.59 |
| Shortness of Breath | 786.05, 786.09 |
| Swollen Legs | 782.3 |
| Nausea | 787.02, 787.01 |
| Decreased Urine Output | 788.5 |
| Palpitations | 785.1 |

### 5. Explainability Layer

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) TreeExplainer computes feature-level attributions for each prediction. For a given patient:

- **Positive SHAP values** → feature increased the risk prediction
- **Negative SHAP values** → feature decreased the risk prediction
- **Magnitude** → strength of influence on the final prediction

Top-5 SHAP features per disease are automatically converted to a natural language explanation using a template-based generator, making attributions accessible to clinical staff without ML expertise.

**Example Output:**
> *"For Type 2 Diabetes, the prediction is driven by: Risk-increasing factors: Average Blood Glucose. Risk-reducing factors: Insulin Prescribed, HbA1c Maximum."*

---

### 6. Longitudinal Analysis

For each hospital admission, a separate feature vector is constructed using only data from that admission (lab results, prescriptions, and diagnoses specific to that HADM_ID). Risk scores are computed per admission and plotted as a time series, revealing the clinical trajectory over the patient's history.

**Risk Zone Classification:**
- 🔴 HIGH RISK: ≥ 70%
- 🟠 MODERATE: 45–70%
- 🟡 LOW-MODERATE: 25–45%
- 🟢 LOW: < 25%

## Ablation Study

A full three-model ablation study was conducted to justify the choice of XGBoost over simpler classifiers on identical feature sets.

| Model | Diabetes AUC | CKD AUC | HF AUC | Diabetes F1 | CKD F1 | HF F1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Logistic Regression | 0.9308 | 0.9507 | 0.9874 | 0.7301 | 0.6514 | 0.9037 |
| Random Forest | 0.9267 | 0.9496 | 0.9859 | 0.7187 | 0.6294 | 0.9053 |
| **XGBoost** | **0.9454** | **0.9623** | **0.9880** | **0.7611** | **0.7098** | **0.9124** |

XGBoost outperforms both baselines across all three diseases and all metrics. The smaller gap on Heart Failure reflects the exceptionally clean signal of cardiac biomarkers (BNP, diuretics, cardiac ICD codes) which even linear models can exploit.

## Installation & Setup

### Prerequisites
- Python 3.11+
- NVIDIA GPU recommended (RTX 3060 or better for BART inference)
- MIMIC-III access credentials from PhysioNet
- ~15GB disk space (MIMIC-III data + BART model weights)

### Step 1 — Clone the Repository
```bash
git clone https://github.com/YOUR_USERNAME/clinicalmind.git
cd clinicalmind
```

### Step 2 — Create Virtual Environment
```bash
python -m venv .venv
.venv\Scripts\activate        # Windows
source .venv/bin/activate     # Linux/Mac
```

### Step 3 — Install Dependencies
```bash
pip install -r requirements.txt
```

### Step 4 — Install PyTorch with CUDA Support
```bash
pip install torch torchvision torchaudio --index-url https://download.pytorch.org/whl/cu121
```

### Step 5 — Add MIMIC-III Data
Place all 10 MIMIC-III CSV files in the `data/` directory. Access requires credentialing at https://physionet.org/content/mimiciii/1.4/

### Step 6 — Save BART Model Locally (One Time)
```bash
python save_model_locally.py
```
Downloads ~1.6GB BART model to `models/bart-large-cnn/` for offline use.

### Step 7 — Run Training Pipeline (One Time)
```bash
python train.py
```
Expected runtime: 5–10 minutes. Trains 3 XGBoost models and saves all preprocessed data.

### Step 8 — Launch Dashboard
```bash
streamlit run app.py
```
Open browser at `http://localhost:8501`

## Limitations

### Technical Limitations

**1. Retrospective Label Overlap**
Disease labels are derived from ICD-9 codes in the same historical record as the training features. In production deployment, training should occur on historical data with labels derived from future diagnoses. The current system is framed as a risk association model, not a prospective predictor.

**2. Single Institution Generalizability**
MIMIC-III data originates from a single academic medical center in Boston between 2001–2012. Performance on data from other institutions, geographic regions, or more recent time periods may differ.

**3. ICU Population Bias**
MIMIC-III exclusively contains ICU admission data. Predictions may not generalize to outpatient, general ward, or primary care populations where disease presentation patterns differ substantially.

**4. Symptom Code Sparsity**
ICD-9 symptom codes (780–799) are infrequently used in ICU settings where diseases are typically already confirmed. Symptom features have low prevalence in the training data, limiting their learned predictive weight.

**5. No Temporal Feature Modeling**
Lab values are aggregated (mean, max, min, std) across all admissions rather than modeled as time series. Clinically important information — such as a rapidly rising creatinine trend — is partially captured but not explicitly modeled.

**6. CHARTEVENTS Exclusion**
Vital signs data (CHARTEVENTS, ~30GB) was excluded due to storage and data quality constraints. This table contains high rates of manually entered erroneous values.

### Ethical Considerations

This system is a research prototype intended for academic demonstration. It is **not validated for clinical use**. All outputs carry a disclaimer requiring physician verification. The system should not be used to make or influence actual clinical decisions.

## Future Work

1. **Temporal Modeling** — Replace statistical aggregation with LSTM or Transformer-based time-series models to capture the sequence and velocity of clinical deterioration
2. **Prospective Validation** — Train on historical admissions and validate on future admissions to demonstrate genuine predictive value
3. **Demographic Bias Analysis** — Systematic evaluation of model performance stratified by age group, gender, and ethnicity
4. **ICD-10 Migration** — Extend label and feature extraction to ICD-10 coded datasets for modern EHR compatibility
5. **Multi-Institution Validation** — Validate on eICU or other public EHR datasets to assess generalizability
6. **Negation-Aware NLP** — Replace keyword-based symptom extraction from clinical notes with MedSpaCy negation-aware NER
7. **Real-Time EHR Integration** — API layer for integration with FHIR-compliant EHR systems (Epic, Cerner)
